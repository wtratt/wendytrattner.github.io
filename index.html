Officially paused Nibble and reflected on my goals, career, and education

September 1, 2020

After building Nibble and deciding to pause it in August, I spent some time reflecting on my career and life goals. I concluded that:

  1. I prefer technical work to nontechnical work
  2. I prefer to work on hardware and innovative technology as opposed to logistics or pure software
  3. I want to have a part in developing innovative climate tech, whether or not I am the founder
  4. I probably still want to start a company at some point, but my immediate priority is becoming a better engineer and learning technical skills so that I can work on hard tech

I also realized my previous lack of commitment to an engineering career path stemmed from insecurity in my technical abilities. These insecurities are rooted in specific instances in my education, such as failing to finish math tests in the allotted time in middle and high school, getting a B on my high school trig test, being one of a handful of women in my high school math, physics and engineering classes, doing most poorly on the math section of standardized tests, and struggling in my math classes at MIT.

These repeated instances of feeling like I was underperforming, coupled with external reinforcement that women aren't as good at math, have led me to the assumption that 'I am bad at math' and that 'I hate math.' The truth is that I am far above average in my mathematical thinking, and that I love and appreciate the way that math allows us to model the world and solve problems. I think a big reason I have 'struggled' in math has nothing to do with my intelligence, but the arbitrary way in which our education system measures our math abilities.

I have never been fast at math. Therefore, I am bad at taking timed tests. Which our education system uses to assess our math abilities. I am not fast, because I triple check my work before I trust it. And given enough time, I will solve almost any problem. If anything, I think that makes me good at math, and well equipped to tackle technical challenges.

Started working on Nibble

June 8, 2020

Nibble is a marketplace for restaurant food that normally goes to waste. Restaurants throw away TONS of good food. It's hard to predict demand, so they tend to prepare more food than they can sell. At the end of the night, the extra food gets tossed. Nibble helps them save money by selling that food at a discount instead of throwing it away.

Interned at Lyft Level 5

Summer 2019

I joined the mechanical analysis team and designed/tested/analyzed camera mounts and acoustics for the autonomous cars. Learned a ton about vibrational analysis and got a lot of CAD practice in Solidworks.

I was hopeful that a future with autonomous cars could improve our society's efficiency and emissions. After learning more about the space, I am less hopeful that this is the future we will actually arrive at. It would require complete adoption of autonomy, one system or company running everything, and a full conversion to electric vehicles. It may still be possible, but is far off in the future. Especially after attending the MIT Energy Initiative's 2020 panel discussing a three year study on the future of mobility I now believe that we should instead focus our passenger vehicle efforts on improving mass public transportation.

Traveled to Peru to test a homemade solar powered drone for rainforest conservation

August, 2018

With a small team of students, I helped developed a solar powered drone prototype in order to improve surveillance of the Amazon rainforest and track deforestation. We met with our partner, Conservation International, in Lima, and then traveled to two rainforest research stations - Villa Carman and Wayquecha - where we tested our drone. The rainforest is absolutely breathtaking. I was so sad to learn that much of the deforestation is driven by poverty. People in need of income cut down small swathes of rainforest in order to start farms, and it's nearly impossible to detect using satellite images.

Went to Italy for a physics internship

January, 2018

Stayed in Benevento for the month while learning about gravitational wave detector physics, specifically focusing on thermal noise within the Virgo and LIGO interferometer mirrors. Did you know these detectors are the most sensitive measurement devices humans have ever built? The detectors pick up the gravitational pull when nearby trees sway in the wind, so the trees around the detectors are cleared just to make the signal less noisy!

Won first place in women's sparring at the Brown ECTC Taekwondo Tournament

November 19, 2017

In World Taekwondo Federation collegiate sparring matches, teams consist of a heavy, medium, and lightweight competitor, divided into belt categories A, B, and C. I was the lightweight competitor on MIT's C1 team. In order to win a match, your team must win 2/3 of weight category pairings. Less than 3 fights occur when one team lacks a third member, or if one team wins the first 2 matches. Due to these rules, most competitors sit out for several matches. At this tournament however, all teams had a lightweight competitor, and I fought every single match. Because I was undefeated, my teammates sat out several matches each. Let's just say it was an exhausting day full of bruising, but extremely fun and well worth the reward!
